Time-domain investigation of a cylindrical acoustic external cloak

S. Guenneau1, B. Lombard2, C. Bellis2 1 UMI 2004 Abraham de Moivre-CNRS, Imperial College London, SW7 2AZ, United Kingdom 2 Aix Marseille Univ, CNRS, Centrale Marseille, LMA, Marseille, France
Abstract

Space folding techniques based on non-monotonic transforms lead to a new class of cylindrical isotropic acoustic cloaks with a constant negative density and a spatially varying negative bulk modulus. We consider an external cloak consisting of a core with a positive definite density matrix and a positive compressibility, and a shell with simultaneously negative density and compressibility. Such a core-shell resonant system creates a virtual folded region outside the shell. To handle such negative physical parameters in the time-domain, a two-step strategy is used: (i) assuming resonant (Drude-type) effective parameters in the frequency-domain; (ii) returning to the time-domain by applying the formalism of the auxiliary fields. We numerically show that, at the designed central frequency, scattering of a cylindrical pressure wave incident upon a finite set of small rigid obstacles is drastically reduced after a lapse of time, when they are placed in the close neighborhood of the external cloak. However, at short times, the external cloak behaves rather like a superscatterer: the cloak itself scatters more than the set of scatterers.

It is by now well established that one can cloak objects surrounded by specially designed anisotropic heterogeneous cloakall ; ulf06 ; oref0 ; greenleaf03 , or plasmonic alu05 , shells. It is even possible to cloak objects using some active sources miller06 ; vasquez09 . In this Letter, our objective is to analyze the possibility to reduce the scattering of small objects placed outside a core-shell system with sign-shifting heterogeneous (yet isotropic) coefficients.

Physically, this means that the acoustic field outside the region of radius R∗subscript𝑅R_{*} should not sense what is inside this region, and this has been confirmed by a mathematical analysis based on the Helmholtz equation in the time-harmonic regime oref16 . Interestingly, the core-shell system (3) can also be viewed as a folded version of the Veselago-Pendry lens neglens ; pendry00 ; milton05 , via the concept of complementary media pendry02 , which is reminiscent of space-folding. Actually, it has been numerically shown in the time-harmonic regime that if one considers a finite size object outside the external cloak and a “space folded” copy of the object consisting of opposite sign electromagnetic parameters inside the shell, then this makes a perfectly transparent electromagnetic core-shell system at any frequency lai09 .

However, despite all these works, the transient behavior of waves propagating across the core-shell system (2) remained an open question until now, and we would like to address it through time-domain numerical simulations. Indeed, we can then make use of the powerful formalism of auxiliary field for electromagnetic gralak10 and acoustic Bellis19 waves propagating within sign-shifting media.

The systems (1) and (6) are solved on a Cartesian computational grid with N1×N2subscript𝑁1subscript𝑁2N_{1}\times N_{2} points. The grid is deemed fine enough with N1=N2=1700subscript𝑁1subscript𝑁21700N_{1}=N_{2}=1700 to ensure a sufficient number of points discretizing the scatterers. A 4-th order space-time ADER scheme is used, together with a splitting algorithm. The discretization of the curved interfaces on the Cartesian grid considered requires care to avoid spurious diffractions and to accurately account for jump conditions. In our first numerical experiment, we consider the cloak only, i.e. without scatterers. Figure 2 illustrates the interaction of the transient waves with the cloak at different time steps. After a transient period (a-b), cloaking takes place (c). The scattered field measurements shown in Figure 5 confirm that cloaking is almost perfect. In (d), a close-up shows waves inside the shell. The effect of space folding can be observed. Dynamic animations show the propagation of negatively refracted waves within the shell 𝒮𝒮\mathcal{S}, where phase and group velocity have an opposite sign since the density ρ𝜌\rho and bulk modulus κ𝜅\kappa are simultaneously negative (albeit being spatially varying) according to (2). However, within the core 𝒞𝒞\mathcal{C}, ρ𝜌\rho and κ𝜅\kappa are both positive constant, but the refractive index therein is larger than in the medium surrounding the cloak, which leads to an enhanced power flow in 𝒞𝒞\mathcal{C}, similar to that in concentrators rahm08 . Finally, anomalous resonances at the core-shell (|𝒙|=Rc𝒙subscript𝑅𝑐|\bm{x}|=R_{c}) and shell-outer medium (|𝒙|=Rs𝒙subscript𝑅𝑠|\bm{x}|=R_{s}) interfaces of such a core-shell system predicted 25 years ago for an electrostatic configuration in oref1 manifest here a wave character since the condition for existence of surface waves akin to those already studied in the context of impedance-matched and sign-shifting electromagnetic media pendry02 ; guenneau05 ; bonnet10 , is naturally satisfied at all times by the principle of space-folding.

Figure 3 illustrates the interaction of transient waves with the scatterers and the cloak, at different time steps. Clearly, at short times, the core-shell system does not cloak the scatterers, and behaves in a way similar to a superscatterer oref12 , whereas at longer times, when some permanent regime has been established, the diffraction by the scatterers is much reduced. A close-up on the scatterers in panel (d) shows the interaction of surface waves, at the matrix-shell interface, with the scatterers in the cloaking region ℱℱ\mathcal{F} .

Figure 4 displays snapshots of the scattered pressure field at t=0.356𝑡0.356t=0.356 s in various configurations. The diffraction patterns of the scatterers (a) are significantly reduced by the cloak (b). In (c), the distance between the cloak and the scatterers is increased to d=4.7𝑑4.7d=4.7 m: the interaction between surface waves and the scatterers is weaker, which degrades the efficiency of the cloak. This degradation is amplified when larger scatterers are considered, as in (d) with R=0.3𝑅0.3R=0.3 m. These act somehow as a screen that prevents waves from reaching the cloak, so that surface waves cannot be generated to reduce scattering. One might argue that a possible route to achieve invisibility for larger scatterers, or even finite size possibly heterogeneous objects, that might be located further away from the cloak, would be to implement an external cloak based on complementary media as in lai09 . But this would imply that the design of the external cloak depends upon the objects to cloak, unlike in the present scheme. We further note that one can in theory increase ad libitum the radius R∗subscript𝑅R_{*} of the cloaking region by considering a vanishing radius Rcsubscript𝑅𝑐R_{c} for the core, which according to (2) requires a core of vanishing bulk modulus. This might open a route to external cloaking of finite size objects, or objects located further away from the cloak.

The coefficient Ω0subscriptΩ0\Omega_{0} in (5) was defined from the frequency f0=100subscript𝑓0100f_{0}=100 Hz. Figure 6 then represents the relative errors measured when the central frequency of the source is shifted as f~0subscript~𝑓0\tilde{f}_{0}. Cloaking performances appear to be significantly reduced when the incident frequency is not tuned so that properties (2) are met within the shell, which highlights the sensitivity of cloaking to the physical parameters used: injecting f=95𝑓95f=95 Hz in (5) yields g​(0.95​ω0)=−1.21𝑔0.95subscript𝜔01.21g(0.95\,\omega_{0})=-1.21, which amounts to a 21 % error in the mass density in (2).

These efficiency measurements as a function of frequency raise two questions: (i) How to emulate negative parameters in the time-domain over a large frequency band? (ii) How to realize such a cloak in practice using a resonant micro-structured medium within the shell? These two questions, crucial to the design of external cloaking in the time-domain, will be the topic of future investigations.
