H. Sebastian Seung
Neuroscience Institute and Computer Science Dept.
Princeton University, Princeton, NJ 08544

Abstract

This paper introduces a rate-based nonlinear neural network in which excitatory (E𝐸E) neurons receive feedforward excitation from sensory (S𝑆S) neurons, and inhibit each other through disynaptic pathways mediated by inhibitory (I𝐼I) interneurons. Correlation-based plasticity of disynaptic inhibition serves to incompletely decorrelate E𝐸E neuron activity, pushing the E𝐸E neurons to learn distinct sensory features. The plasticity equations additionally contain “extra” terms fostering competition between excitatory synapses converging onto the same postsynaptic neuron and inhibitory synapses diverging from the same presynaptic neuron. The parameters of competition between S→E→𝑆𝐸S\to E connections can be adjusted to make learned features look more like “parts” or “wholes.” The parameters of competition between I−E𝐼𝐸I-E connections can be adjusted to set the typical decorrelatedness and sparsity of E𝐸E neuron activity. Numerical simulations of unsupervised learning show that relatively few I𝐼I neurons can be sufficient for achieving good decorrelation, and increasing the number of I𝐼I neurons makes decorrelation more complete. Excitatory and inhibitory inputs to active E𝐸E neurons are approximately balanced as a result of learning.

Network models combining Hebbian excitation and anti-Hebbian inhibition have been explored previously by numerous researchers. The model of Földiák (1990) contained neurons that were recurrently connected via all-to-all lateral inhibition, and received feedforward connections from sensory afferents. Anti-Hebbian plasticity of inhibitory connections forced the neurons to decorrelate their activities, thereby enabling the Hebbian feedforward connections to learn distinct sensory features. The term “anti-Hebbian inhibition” was used by Földiák (1990) to mean that correlated activity leads to strengthening of an inhibitory connection (a negative number becomes more negative).

King et al. (2013) studied a model containing excitatory (E𝐸E) neurons reciprocally connected with inhibitory (I𝐼I) neurons, and also receiving connections from sensory (S𝑆S) afferents (Fig. 1). The S→E→𝑆𝐸S\to E connections were Hebbian, as in the Földiák (1990) model. The E𝐸E neurons did not inhibit each other directly, but indirectly through disynaptic E→I→E→𝐸𝐼→𝐸E\to I\to E pathways mediated by I𝐼I neurons. This network motif will be called “disynaptic recurrent inhibition,” or just “disynaptic inhibition.” The E→I→𝐸𝐼E\to I connections were modified by Hebbian plasticity and the I→E→𝐼𝐸I\to E connections by anti-Hebbian plasticity. King et al. (2013) showed that the I𝐼I neurons could force the E𝐸E neurons to decorrelate their activities, enabling Hebbian plasticity of feedforward connections to learn distinct sensory features much as in the original Földiák (1990) model.

This paper introduces a rate-based variant of the King et al. (2013) model, which used spiking integrate-and-fire model neurons for neurobiological realism. A rate-based model is more amenable to mathematical understanding. A rate-based model is also potentially useful for machine learning applications, as it does not require the computational overhead of simulating a spiking network. The novelty of the present model relative to previous rate-based models is that it is (1) nonlinear, and (2) separated into distinct E𝐸E and I𝐼I populations that respect Dale’s Law.111Here Dale’s Law means that the outgoing synapses of a neuron are typically either all excitatory or all inhibitory. The original formulation of Dale’s Law is that a neuron secretes a single neurotransmitter at all of its outgoing synapses. The formulations are equivalent if a single neurotransmitter has uniformly excitatory or uniformly inhibitory influences on all postsynaptic neurons. There are known exceptions in which a neuron secretes a single neurotransmitter that excites some postsynaptic neurons and inhibits others, such as photoreceptor synapses onto retinal bipolar cells (Euler et al., 2014), or when a neuron secretes more than one neurotransmitter (Vaaga et al., 2014). Dale’s Law is also known as Dale’s Principle. Some previous rate-based models were nonlinear but did not contain separate E𝐸E and I𝐼I populations (Földiák, 1990; Hu et al., 2014; Pehlevan and Chklovskii, 2014; Seung and Zung, 2017). Other previous rate-based models had linear neurons that performed principal component analysis, and separated principal neurons and interneurons without respecting Dale’s Law (Plumbley, 1993; Fyfe, 1995; Pehlevan and Chklovskii, 2015).

A first payoff of mathematical tractability is that the plasticity equation for I−E𝐼𝐸I-E connections can be “derived” from the Földiák (1990) model, or more precisely from the variant due to Seung and Zung (2017). The derivation results in a Hebbian/anti-Hebbian plasticity equation plus two “extra” terms fostering synaptic competition. Competitive interactions between synapses sharing the same presynaptic or postsynaptic neuron have long been postulated by models of cortical development, and are supported by neurobiological evidence (Miller, 1996). Surprisingly, the derivation suggests that the parameters of I−E𝐼𝐸I-E synaptic competition set the typical decorrelatedness and sparseness of E𝐸E activity.

The derivation regards disynaptic inhibition as an approximate matrix factorization of all-to-all inhibition.222The “derivation” may not really be mathematically justifiable, because it involves approximating a Lagrange multiplier. More precise mathematical justifications are given in a companion paper (Seung, 2018). Due to the approximation, decorrelation is expected to be incomplete, with more I𝐼I neurons leading to more complete decorrelation. Numerical simulations of unsupervised learning from MNIST images of handwritten digits show that good decorrelation is achieved with only a modest number of I𝐼I neurons, similar to what was already reported by King et al. (2013). A novelty here is that good decorrelation is achievable (at least in this example) without I→I→𝐼𝐼I\to I connections, which are lacking in the present model but exist in King et al. (2013).

The mathematical form of competition between S→E→𝑆𝐸S\to E connections is not fixed by the derivation, and for simplicity is chosen to be the same as for I−E𝐼𝐸I-E connections. Competition between S→E→𝑆𝐸S\to E connections determines the sparsity of connectivity, and therefore whether learned features look more like “parts” or “wholes.”

Synaptic competition is linear in the connection strenths; the only nonlinearity is the nonnegativity constraint. This simplicity lends itself to mathematical analysis. The “winners” of synaptic competition are connections to neurons with the most strongly correlated activities; other connections vanish. The number of surviving nonzero connections depends on the numerical parameters of synaptic competition, as well as the values of the activity correlations. Adjusting the parameters makes competition more or less “winner-take-all,” resulting in sparser or fuller connectivity, respectively.

Numerical simulations show that the network exhibits approximate excitatory-inhibitory balance after learning, in the sense that active E𝐸E cells receive excitatory input that only slightly exceeds inhibitory input. This has an intriguing correspondence with the excitatory-inhibitory balance observed in cortical circuits (Isaacson and Scanziani, 2011). Previous models of balanced networks have relied upon nonmodifiable random or structured connectivity (Denève and Machens, 2016), rather than connections shaped by plasticity.

1 Network model: description and “derivation”

In the network model, E𝐸E neurons receive excitatory input from S𝑆S neurons and inhibit each other through disynaptic pathways mediated by I𝐼I neurons (Fig. 1). The plasticity equation for I−E𝐼𝐸I-E connections is “derived” from a previous model in which neurons monosynaptically inhibit each other through all-to-all connections (Földiák, 1990; Seung and Zung, 2017). In the previous model, anti-Hebbian inhibition served to decorrelate the activity of the neurons. In the present model, anti-Hebbian I𝐼I neurons serve to imperfectly decorrelate the activities of E𝐸E neurons.

2.1 The E𝐸E neurons learn sensory features

For each of the 64 E𝐸E neurons, the set of convergent S→E→𝑆𝐸S\to E connections can be viewed as an image (Fig. 2). The images will be called “sensory features,” or simply “features.” For the base parameter configuration (γ/κ=5𝛾𝜅5\gamma/\kappa=5), the features are more sparse and resemble character “strokes.” For γ/κ=50𝛾𝜅50\gamma/\kappa=50, the features are more full, and look almost like entire digits. By controlling sparsity of S→E→𝑆𝐸S\to E connections, the ratio γ/κ𝛾𝜅\gamma/\kappa effectively determines whether E𝐸E neurons learn “parts” or “wholes.”

2.2 E neurons are more selective than I neurons

At the beginning of learning, all E𝐸E neurons are active when the network dynamics (1) converges to a steady state. As learning proceeds, some neurons become inactive at the steady state (Fig. 3). Once learning has converged, E𝐸E activity is sparse at the steady state, while all I𝐼I neurons are active for all stimuli (Fig. 4). The activity of E𝐸E neurons is more sparse for smaller values of p/q𝑝𝑞p/q (Figs. 3 and 4).

2.3 Decorrelation is incomplete

By the arguments of Section 1.2, we expect that decorrelation should be less complete when I𝐼I cells are less numerous. Indeed, reducing to a single I𝐼I neuron results in a long tail of highly correlated E𝐸E cell pairs (Fig. 6). The tail is reduced in the base parameter configuration of 555 I𝐼I neurons, and further reduced when the number of I𝐼I neurons is increased to 10 (Fig. 6).888One might expect that complete decorrelation is guaranteed at r=n𝑟𝑛r=n (full rank). However this is not necessarily true as A𝐴A is constrained to be nonnegative.

3 Synaptic competition

The first terms of the plasticity equations (3) and (4) are nonnegative, since all neural activities are nonnegative. Therefore the connection strengths could increase without bound, were it not for the “extra” terms in the plasticity equations. In the plasticity equation (3) for Δ​Wi​aΔsubscript𝑊𝑖𝑎\Delta W_{ia}, the “weight decay” term −γ​Wi​a𝛾subscript𝑊𝑖𝑎-\gamma W_{ia} is said to be homosynaptic, since the change in the connection depends on the strength of the same connection. The other term −κ​∑bWi​b𝜅subscript𝑏subscript𝑊𝑖𝑏-\kappa\sum_{b}W_{ib} is heterosynaptic, since through it Δ​Wi​aΔsubscript𝑊𝑖𝑎\Delta W_{ia} depends on all other connections converging onto the i𝑖ith E𝐸E cell.999In models of cortical development, it is more common to constrain the sums in Eqs. (3) and (4), so that for example ∑bWi​b=ρsubscript𝑏subscript𝑊𝑖𝑏𝜌\sum_{b}W_{ib}=\rho (Von der Malsburg, 1973; Miller and MacKay, 1994). This has the biological interpretation that the synapses are competing for fixed amount of resources. The constraint can be implemented in a “soft” way by substituting ∑bWi​b−ρsubscript𝑏subscript𝑊𝑖𝑏𝜌\sum_{b}W_{ib}-\rho for ∑bWi​bsubscript𝑏subscript𝑊𝑖𝑏\sum_{b}W_{ib} in Eq. (3), as was done by Seung and Zung (2017). Here the constraint is eliminated altogether to reduce the number of parameters and simplify the model.

The roles of the two terms can be explained with a financial analogy. The weight decay term −γ​Wi​a𝛾subscript𝑊𝑖𝑎-\gamma W_{ia} is larger for stronger connections, and therefore acts like a “flat tax.” The heterosynaptic term −κ​∑aWi​a𝜅subscript𝑎subscript𝑊𝑖𝑎-\kappa\sum_{a}W_{ia} is the same for all connections, large or small, converging onto neuron i𝑖i. This amounts to a “regressive tax” on convergent connections. By itself the regressive tax would lead to “winner-take-all”: all connections would vanish except for a single winner. The flat tax is more egalitarian: adding it prevents a single winner from dominating.

An analogous combination of homosynaptic and heterosynaptic terms is also found in the plasticity equation (4) for Δ​Aα​iΔsubscript𝐴𝛼𝑖\Delta A_{\alpha i}. In this case the heterosynaptic term fosters competition between inhibitory connections diverging from the α𝛼\alphath I𝐼I neuron and excitatory connections converging onto the α𝛼\alphath I𝐼I neuron.

Competition between synapses is simple enough for analytical treatment. Eq. (4) and nonnegativity of A𝐴A imply that

(q2−p2)​Aα​j≈[⟨yα​xj⟩−p2​∑iAα​i]+superscript𝑞2superscript𝑝2subscript𝐴𝛼𝑗superscriptdelimited-[]delimited-⟨⟩subscript𝑦𝛼subscript𝑥𝑗superscript𝑝2subscript𝑖subscript𝐴𝛼𝑖\left(q^{2}-p^{2}\right)A_{\alpha j}\approx\left[\langle y_{\alpha}x_{j}\rangle-p^{2}\sum_{i}A_{\alpha i}\right]^{+} (12)

at a stationary state of learning. Numerical simulations (Fig. 7) agree well with Eq. (12). For each I𝐼I neuron α𝛼\alpha, the top k𝑘k connections are linearly related to the top k𝑘k correlations, while the rest of the connections vanish. The number k𝑘k of surviving connections varies from neuron to neuron. It depends on the ratio p/q𝑝𝑞p/q as well as the values of the activity correlations through inequalities derived in Appendix B. Simple statements can be made for limiting cases of p/q𝑝𝑞p/q. The competition is “winner-take-all” as p/q→1→𝑝𝑞1p/q\to 1, with a single nonzero connection surviving for each I𝐼I neuron. As p/q→0→𝑝𝑞0p/q\to 0, on the other hand, all connections survive.

Visualization of the E−I𝐸𝐼E-I connections from numerical simulations (Fig. 8) illustrates that a fuller A𝐴A matrix corresponds to sparser E𝐸E activity. Small p/q𝑝𝑞p/q means weaker competition and less sparse A𝐴A connections. At the same time, it means sparser and more decorrelated E𝐸E activity, by Eq. (11).

For each E𝐸E neuron i𝑖i, the top k𝑘k connections are linearly related to the top k𝑘k correlations, while the rest of the connections vanish. The number k𝑘k of surviving connections varies from neuron to neuron, and is governed by an inequality derived in Appendix B.

4 Excitatory-inhibitory balance

At a steady state of the activity dynamics, Eq. (1) implies that

xi=[λi−1​(∑a=1mWi​a​ua−∑α=1ryα​Aα​i)]+subscript𝑥𝑖superscriptdelimited-[]superscriptsubscript𝜆𝑖1superscriptsubscript𝑎1𝑚subscript𝑊𝑖𝑎subscript𝑢𝑎superscriptsubscript𝛼1𝑟subscript𝑦𝛼subscript𝐴𝛼𝑖x_{i}=\left[\lambda_{i}^{-1}\left(\sum_{a=1}^{m}W_{ia}u_{a}-\sum_{\alpha=1}^{r}y_{\alpha}A_{\alpha i}\right)\right]^{+}

The activity of the i𝑖ith E𝐸E cell is determined by the difference between its excitatory input λi−1​∑aWi​a​uasuperscriptsubscript𝜆𝑖1subscript𝑎subscript𝑊𝑖𝑎subscript𝑢𝑎\lambda_{i}^{-1}\sum_{a}W_{ia}u_{a} (due to S→E→𝑆𝐸S\to E connections) and its inhibitory input λi−1​∑αyα​Aα​isuperscriptsubscript𝜆𝑖1subscript𝛼subscript𝑦𝛼subscript𝐴𝛼𝑖\lambda_{i}^{-1}\sum_{\alpha}y_{\alpha}A_{\alpha i} (due to I→E→𝐼𝐸I\to E connections). Active E𝐸E cells are those for which excitatory input exceeds inhibitory input. Numerical simulations show that excitatory input only slightly exceeds inhibitory input for active cells (Fig. 10). This is reminiscent of a phenomenon reported for cortical neurons and known as excitatory-inhibitory balance (Isaacson and Scanziani, 2011). Previous computational models of balanced networks have focused on explaining Poisson-like variability of spiking activity (Denève and Machens, 2016). The current network is not intended as an explanation of spiking variability, as it is a rate-based model. The novelty is that excitatory-inhibitory balance emerges from synaptic plasticity rather than the nonmodifiable random or structured connectivity of previous models (Denève and Machens, 2016).

5 Discussion

Numerical simulations have shown that a modest number of I𝐼I neurons can be sufficient to decorrelate the activities of E𝐸E neurons, consistent with the fact that inhibitory neurons are a small minority of cortical neurons. Rather complete decorrelation is achievable without I−I𝐼𝐼I-I connections. Including I−I𝐼𝐼I-I connections, as in the prior spiking network model of King et al. (2013), would add more neurobiological realism as well as computational power. Future work will consider the computational roles of E−E𝐸𝐸E-E as well as I−I𝐼𝐼I-I connections.

Many classes of inhibitory neurons are known to exist in the cortex. King et al. (2013) proposed that the anti-Hebbian I𝐼I neurons in their model should be identified with parvalbumin-positive fast-spiking basket cells, which are fast, have high average firing rates, are reciprocally coupled with excitatory cells, and exhibit plasticity. This class of cells is also a reasonable candidate for the I𝐼I neurons in the present model.

The number of connections in a network with disynaptic inhibition is much reduced relative to a network with all-to-all inhibition. Therefore disynaptic inhibition can be regarded as a more efficient way of achieving decorrelation. In this view, incompleteness of decorrelation is something to be eliminated or reduced as much as possible. Alternatively, one can imagine that incompleteness of decorrelation could have an important computational function. Perhaps the remaining correlations in the output of the network could serve as a basis for further learning by another network.
